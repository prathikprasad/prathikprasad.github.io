KARVIMIO: Hands-Free AR Assembly Guidance for the Operating Room

The Vision: Replacing Paper Manuals with Sterile Spatial Guidance

Context & Opportunity

In high-stakes medical environments, the flawless preparation and assembly of complex surgical instruments is critical to patient safety and operational efficiency. Surgical staff are frequently required to assemble specialized, rarely used tools under immense pressure.

At the Human-Centered Computing and Extended Reality (HEX) Lab at the Technical University of Munich, in collaboration with Klinikum rechts der Isar, we initiated KARVIMIO to address this workflow bottleneck.

My Role: XR Interaction Designer & Researcher

I conceptualized, designed, and evaluated spatial interfaces that could replace paper manuals without compromising sterility or focus in the operating room.

The system used HoloLens 2, AI-based object recognition, and markerless 6D pose estimation to deliver context-aware instructions directly into the user's field of view.

The Problem in Depth: Paper is Non-Sterile

The current standard for surgical instrument assembly relies heavily on paper-based manuals. In the operating room, this creates a severe usability flaw: a scrub nurse cannot touch paper without compromising sterility.

As a result, the nurse must rely on a second, non-sterile team member to hold the manual and turn pages. This dependency consumes time from two skilled professionals and distracts from the immediate task.

The Cognitive Gap: 2D Schematics vs. 3D Reality

Translating 2D technical schematics into 3D physical actions requires high cognitive effort. In a dense OR setup with multiple instrument parts scattered across sterile tables, identifying the correct component and orientation becomes a stress-inducing process.

We needed a solution that eliminated physical interaction with instructional materials while providing unambiguous spatial context.

Research & Discovery: A 32-Person Within-Subject Study

To systematically evaluate AR guidance, we designed a within-subject user study with 32 participants: 16 medical experts with OR experience and 16 laypeople. Participants assembled four 3D-printed instruments of varying complexity using four guidance methods: paper baseline, marker-fixed AR, field-of-view fixed AR, and in-situ AR.

We structured the evaluation around the General Assembly Task Model (GATM), which breaks assembly into measurable blocks: locating a part, picking it up, finding its correct orientation, and executing the final assembly. We collected objective metrics such as task completion time and error rates, alongside NASA-TLX, System Usability Scale, and User Experience Questionnaire feedback.

1

t-locate

User scans the workspace to find the required component. Dense visual clutter makes small, similarly colored parts difficult to identify.

2

t-pick

User physically grasps the targeted component. Ambiguous highlighting can lead to accidentally picking the wrong part.

3

t-locate-pos

User determines the correct spatial orientation and destination for the part.

4

t-assemble

User connects the component to the main assembly housing while hands may occlude the AR guide.

5

Feedback

Participants evaluated cognitive load, usability, and the clarity of each AR visualization method.

Participants praised the hands-free nature of AR. One lay participant described it as "intuitive, more time-saving than traditional manuals as well as independent of a second person." But the in-situ method revealed a critical flaw: when white holograms overlapped white physical components, exact orientation was hard to read.

Framing the Challenge: Guidance Without Obstruction

The core design challenge became clear: how do we provide precise, context-aware 3D guidance without digital overlays obstructing the user's view of the physical task?

The OR constraints meant we could not rely on QR codes or physical markers because they compromise sterility. We had to use markerless 6D pose estimation, which introduced latency and spatial offset.

Technical Constraints: Markerless Tracking in Practice

A top-down Azure Kinect camera tracked instruments and sent RGB and depth data to a server running AI processing at 16 frames per second. The resulting 6D pose data was transmitted by Wi-Fi to the HoloLens 2.

This offloaded heavy processing, but introduced 60 ms latency and a pose estimation error ranging from 0.5 cm to 2 cm. For millimeter-precise surgical tools, that offset could make in-situ holograms feel misaligned.

Design Exploration: Rendering for Clarity

We prototyped six visual shaders for AR overlays: solid, semi-transparent, wireframe plus fill, standard wireframe, Fresnel, and silhouette. The goal was to balance structural context with real-world visibility.

Solid models looked impressive in isolation but failed in practice because they blocked the user's grip. Standard wireframes kept the physical hands visible but lacked enough depth information for precise assembly.

Iteration: Wireframe plus Translucent Fill

A wireframe with light translucent fill gave the best combination of structural cues and visibility. But placement mattered as much as material. We tested in-situ, FoV-fixed, and marker-fixed spatial paradigms.

In-situ felt the most futuristic, but the 0.5-2 cm tracking offset made it mentally taxing. Marker-fixed and FoV-fixed guidance kept the work surface clearer and gave users a stable point of reference.

Interaction Design: Hands-Free Control

Physical interaction with the UI was prohibited, so we designed a gaze-controlled interface. Large Previous and Next buttons were anchored spatially within the user's environment.

To prevent accidental triggers while users scanned a busy table, the system used a deliberate 3-second dwell time. A progressive visual fill and concluding chime confirmed navigation.

Reducing Cognitive Load: Locate First, Assemble Second

We separated picking instructions from assembly instructions. First, a color-coded floating tooltip with a directional arrow pointed to the physical object to pick up. Then, once the part was identified, the system showed the 3D animation explaining how it connected to the main assembly.

This reduced visual clutter and gave each step a clear purpose.

A close-up of the AR UI with large Next and Previous buttons. The Next button is partially filled to show the 3-second gaze dwell interaction.

Gaze-Controlled Navigation

We finalized marker-fixed mode as the primary fallback because it kept the user's central field of view unobstructed. The instruction UI and animations stayed in a static, world-fixed position above the workspace, making the table easier to read and reducing the mental cost of tracking jitter.

The final experience blends spatial computing and computer vision. Impact & Results: Stability Beat Spectacle

The 32-person study validated the design direction. AR methods scored higher in user experience, especially in stimulation and novelty, while marker-fixed AR achieved the strongest usability outcome.

Medical experts made significantly fewer assembly errors than laypeople and recognized the value of the system for training. Expert feedback reinforced the decision to keep the table clear, while participants noted that graphic representation of the parts to be picked helped a lot.

Despite the appeal of in-situ animations, the data showed that users had to compensate for tracking offsets. By prioritizing stability and clear visibility over flashy overlays, we delivered a tool better suited to medical professionals.

Reflection: Designing for Mission-Critical AR

KARVIMIO was a lesson in the realities of deploying spatial computing in high-stakes environments. What looks best in a concept video, such as holograms perfectly overlaying physical objects, can hinder performance if the tracking is not flawless.

The 0.5-2 cm offset showed that graceful degradation is essential. A stable, slightly offset reference can be more useful than a jittery overlay that tries to be perfectly aligned.

Next Steps: Handling Occlusion

The next iteration will focus on refining AI tracking to handle severe hand occlusions, which remains the biggest technical hurdle.

We also plan to explore context-aware UI that automatically transitions from solid to wireframe rendering as the user's hand approaches the assembly point.

Evaluating Hands-Free Gaze-Driven Selection for Surgical AR

The Next Question: How Should Sterile Users Select AR Instructions?

Context & Opportunity

After establishing KARVIMIO as an AR assistance system for surgical instrument assembly, the next challenge was interaction. Scrub nurses perform complex bimanual tasks and cannot touch controllers, screens, or hand-tracking buttons without violating sterility.

Voice commands were also unsuitable because operating rooms are noisy and speech recognition failures can become hazardous in a high-pressure clinical workflow.

My Role: XR Interaction Designer & Researcher

I designed, prototyped, and evaluated gaze-driven selection techniques for Magic Leap 2 using Unity 2022.3.30f1 and MRTK 2.8.3.0.

The goal was to benchmark eye-only selection against multimodal baselines and identify an interaction model that maximized precision while minimizing physical and cognitive workload.

A high-fidelity photograph showing a scrub nurse in a sterile operating room wearing a Magic Leap 2 AR headset, with surgical instruments on a table and an overhead camera for AI object detection.

The KARVIMIO Operating Room Setup

The Problem in Depth: The Midas Touch Dilemma

Gaze interfaces must distinguish between a user simply looking at an element and intentionally selecting it. Continuous head movement and long dwell times can solve this technically, but they create physical fatigue during long procedures.

Scrub nurses work under intense temporal demand, so even small interaction delays can disrupt surgical flow.

Baseline Trade-Offs: Foot Pedal vs. Eye-Only Input

Foot pedals are common in medical contexts and provide mechanical certainty, but they add hardware, limit mobility, and can introduce tripping hazards.

Eye-only inputs such as blinking and winking require no external hardware, but depend heavily on headset sensor accuracy and can create false positives.

Research & Discovery: Benchmarking Four Selection Techniques

We ran a within-subject study using a Latin-square design to balance condition ordering. The 32 participants were split into 16 professional scrub nurses and 16 non-medical laypeople, allowing us to isolate how domain expertise changes workload perception and usability expectations.

Participants completed a precise 3D object alignment task using four selection techniques: 2-second dwell time, double-blink, double-wink, and a physical foot pedal baseline. We logged task completion time and per-selection interaction time, then collected NASA-TLX, SUS, and UEQ feedback.

The results challenged our assumptions. The foot pedal produced the strongest overall experience, while double-wink was the fastest individual selection technique. But the same wink interaction also increased mental demand and exposed hardware detection instability.

Framing the Challenge: Reliability Over Novelty

Medical professionals were significantly more critical of temporal delays and system errors than laypeople. In this context, UI friction is not just annoying; it can threaten procedure flow.

Asymmetric facial gestures such as winking were fast, but caused higher mental demand and were prone to detection errors. Some participants reported that the system interpreted too much input as blinking.

Design Direction: Bridge Hardware Certainty and Gaze Freedom

The challenge became bridging the mechanical reliability of a foot pedal with the hardware-free convenience of gaze-only input.

Dwell-based selection was slow, but dependable. The interaction framework needed to support multimodal input, using the foot pedal for primary selection while keeping dwell time as a sterile fallback when mobility was restricted.

Design Exploration: A Controlled Alignment Interface

We designed a point-and-select 3D alignment task to isolate selection technique performance from navigation complexity. The interface used a movable red 3D object that had to be aligned with a white target object initially spaced 6 cm apart.

Users controlled movement along X, Y, and Z axes through directional buttons arranged in a cross pattern.

Visual Feedback: Reticle, Hover, Chime

Directional controls were placed in semi-transparent blue and purple containers with high-contrast white iconography. A small white circular reticle continuously showed the user's estimated gaze position.

When gaze intersected with a button, the container highlighted. Each successful selection moved the 3D object by 1 cm and played a distinct chime.

The Solution: A Testable Multimodal AR Selection Framework

The final prototype integrated all four selection modalities into one standardized interface. By keeping the visual layout and task mechanics constant, the study isolated the input method as the primary variable.

Impact & Results: Definitive Benchmarks for Sterile AR Input

The foot pedal emerged as the superior method for overall usability, with a SUS score of 74.69, the lowest frustration rating, and the fastest total task completion time at 25.43 seconds.

Eye-only methods revealed a more nuanced trade-off. Double-wink achieved the fastest per-selection speed at 1.85 seconds, but tracking inaccuracies and false positives stretched total task time to 30.53 seconds and increased mental demand.

The study proved that 100% hands-free AR navigation is feasible, but the optimal selection trigger depends heavily on context, hardware reliability, and the clinical tolerance for false positives.

Reflection: Mechanical Reliability Still Matters

The main takeaway was the difference in technology tolerance between domains. Designing for laypeople allows a margin of error that medical professionals working under cognitive load cannot accept.

In surgical UI design, mechanical reliability often beats theoretical interaction speed. Robust, low-fatigue fallbacks are more valuable than novel but fragile input modalities.

Next Steps: Adaptive and Predictive Input

Future iterations could explore adaptive dwell times that respond to cognitive load and task phase.

AI-based predictive tracking could also smooth noisy blink and wink data, potentially helping eye-only techniques approach the foot pedal's reliability. Longitudinal studies are needed to measure visual fatigue over multi-hour surgical procedures.
